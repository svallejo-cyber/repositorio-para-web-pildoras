From an overgrown catalog to a portfolio with judgment

Author: Iván Morales

Core idea: build a tool able to analyze technical features, pricing, market penetration, and commercial performance of each reference in order to detect overlap, cannibalization, and real portfolio simplification opportunities.

The goal is not only to reduce references. It is to organize the catalog so each product has a clear positioning and the company can make better economic, technical, and strategic decisions.

The problem

After years of growth and reference accumulation, the company's catalog has become too extensive. As Iván explains, this creates overlap areas where different products compete without enough differentiation in performance, price, or channel. The likely result is internal complexity, commercial inefficiency, and cannibalization.

What the pill proposes

The proposal is to build a portfolio analysis tool that crosses sales data, technical information, and company-defined criteria in order to obtain a much clearer reading of the catalog.

Diagnosis

Identify overlapping products, positioning gaps, and possible inconsistencies between range, channel, and price.

Decision

Provide criteria to decide what to keep, review, and rationalize without putting a relevant share of revenue at risk.

Speed

Reduce analysis time dramatically versus a manual reference-by-reference review.

Scalability

Prepare a base that can connect with margin and profitability analyses already underway inside the company.

Why AI fits here

AI does not replace business judgment. It accelerates it. In this case it would allow large volumes of data to be analyzed under company-defined premises and returned as a much more useful visual representation of catalog problems and opportunities.

Its value lies in making viable an analysis that would otherwise be slow, dispersed, and difficult to sustain over time.

Strategic reading

This piece points to a structural and relevant problem: when a catalog grows without a rigorous periodic reading, the company may end up sustaining complexity that does not contribute proportional value. The proposal seeks to turn that review into a recurrent analytical capability, not a one-off exercise.

Preliminary conclusion: the opportunity lies in moving from a broad but difficult-to-govern catalog to a much more intelligible portfolio, where technical performance, sales, price, and profitability can be read together to support better decisions.
