From invoice to management criteria: how to build a useful energy analysis system for the store network

In many organizations with a broad territorial footprint, utility information already exists (electricity, water, and so on), but not always in a form that allows it to be managed and analyzed properly. Three- or four-page invoices filled with data that is not intuitive for an ordinary user arrive, are archived, are paid, and are reviewed only when an obvious deviation in cost appears. The problem is that there is a significant gap between having invoices and having management criteria. This project is precisely about closing that gap: turning a large mass of data into a reading tool that is useful, comparable and oriented to analysis and decision-making.

The organization has a broad network of stores and each utility supply generates monthly invoices with consumption data, amounts, periods, contracted power, maximum demand and many other regulatory concepts. On paper, all that information is already available. In practice, reviewing it jointly is slow, inconsistent and highly dependent on manual reading that does not scale well.

A dual approach emerged from that need. On the one hand, it was necessary to build a reliable PDF extraction process, because the source data was not in a structured database but inside invoices with different formats, dirty text and complex blocks. On the other, that extraction had to be transformed into an interface that allowed the utilities estate to be analyzed in a way that was useful for the business. The question was not only how much we spend, but where the deviations are, which stores show abnormal behavior, which supplies have oversized contracted power and where it makes sense to dig deeper first. The tool was built precisely with that logic: reduce mechanical reading and increase the capacity for judgment, while always maintaining the link to the original invoice so the conclusion can be reviewed.

The reviewer can now enter, see the information by periods and tariffs, inspect a specific store and its evolution, compare it with others, review an alert, open the PDF behind it and understand in a few minutes whether there is a real problem, an administrative anomaly or a savings opportunity.

This top-down approach helps ensure that each question has its place: what is happening overall, what requires urgent review and where it is worth going into detail.

Without giving more prominence than necessary to a simple tool, I would like to share a few reflections that came up while writing this text.

- Any excuse is a good one. The idea emerged from a meeting with a third party for which we had very little preparation time: we needed, very quickly, a clear view of the utilities of a company that was far from simple. The data had existed for years, but it had not been properly worked or structured. It took only a few seconds to think of AI as the best ally to help us. The tool took only a few seconds to process around 1,500 invoices and return the relevant information. In the many documents I reviewed to validate the data and be able to trust it, I found no errors. Seeing its potential, what started as a one-off solution to get four fast answers was taken a step further and developed into what I hope can become a useful tool for the company.

- Professionalizing in a scalable way: efficiency vs. improvement. This is an AI use case with a very different objective from the one often raised in the public debate. It is not about replacing people with machines, but about enabling a new task that was previously not viable because of volume, time or review cost. In this case, the tool helps professionalize the company’s energy management in a scalable way. We now have fast, precise and traceable information, whether we are talking about five stores or one hundred.

- Speed in the service of judgment. Professional judgment remains essential. Technology does not decide what to do on its own; what it does is prepare the ground better for decision-making. In fact, I believe my contribution here was not building the tool itself, but identifying a need, knowing how to structure it, which data to use, which indicators are relevant and which are anomalous.

Ultimately, the value lies in mastering the what and the how; AI takes care of multiplying it.
