From tender document to useful data: assisted tender reading

Luis Merelo · First collaborative contribution to the hub on document automation oriented to business opportunity

Context

This piece starts from a very concrete need: reducing time and friction in the initial review of tenders by turning long, heterogeneous documents into a clear and useful output for decision-making.

The goal was not simply to read PDFs, but to convert complex documentation into a structured, business-oriented reading.

The operational problem

Each file requires the rapid identification of critical information: what is being requested, which items appear, which amounts matter, and which requirements condition participation.

The difficulty lies in the mix of document types, the poor quality of extracted text, and the need to distinguish useful signals from misleading matches.

What the project is trying to solve

Speed

Reduce the time needed to read and filter tender files in the early stages.

Shared criteria

Read different documents with the same structure and the same business logic.

Useful signal

Detect relevant items and amounts for paints, ETICS, fireproofing and other lines of interest.

Less friction

Remove mechanical work without replacing the professional judgment of the person reviewing the opportunity.

Key difficulties found

  • High variability between technical specifications, administrative conditions, tender notices and unrelated documents.
  • PDF outputs with noise, badly interpreted tables, repeated headers and weak OCR.
  • The need to move from simple keyword searches to a more contextual and validated reading.
  • The importance of knowing when a match is truly a useful item and when it should be discarded.

Contribution to the organization

The tool improves the speed of preliminary opportunity analysis, brings consistency and reduces dependence on a fully manual first review.

It also turns dispersed knowledge about what we should look for in a tender into an explicit, reusable and improvable operational criterion.

Collaborative conclusion: the real value is not in digitizing documents, but in translating complex documentation into faster, clearer and better grounded decisions.
