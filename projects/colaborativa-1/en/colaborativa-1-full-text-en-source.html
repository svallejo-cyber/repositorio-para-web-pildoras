From tender document to useful data: how we are building an assisted tender reading system

In many organizations, tender review remains a time-intensive task, repetitive in nature and exposed to interpretation errors. Each file requires the rapid identification of key information, determining whether it fits the company’s activity and answering very specific questions: what is being requested, which items appear, which amounts are relevant and which requirements condition participation. The idea behind this project arises precisely from that operational need: to transform long, heterogeneous documents, often difficult to read, into a clear, comparable and useful output for decision-making.

The starting point was a very practical need. It was not just about “reading PDFs”, but about turning specifications, notices and administrative documentation into a structured, business-oriented reading. The organization needed to know quickly whether a tender included items related to paints, ETICS, fireproofing, coatings, flooring, carpet, waterproofing or laminates, and also detect whether there were amounts of significant economic weight. In other words, extracting text was not enough: the document had to be interpreted through a commercial and operational opportunity logic.

As development progressed, problems emerged that are common in this type of initiative, but that are only properly understood when working with real documentation. The first was the enormous documentary variability. Under the same flow there could be technical specifications, administrative specifications, tender notices and even documents that were not tenders at all, such as invoices or delivery notes. A system that was too rigid confused document types; one that was too flexible generated false positives. This was critical, because poor initial classification drags errors through the entire analysis chain.

The second problem was the quality of the extracted text. Many PDFs contain strange line breaks, poorly interpreted tables, repeated headers, OCR noise or administrative structures that make it difficult to locate the truly useful data. In the first analyses, we observed merged amounts, incorrectly captured public body names and misleading matches caused simply by the appearance of one word inside another. This forced us to move from a basic keyword search to a more careful logic based on context, text cleaning, business rules and validation against real examples.

The third problem is the most relevant from a functional point of view: answering the mandatory questions well does not simply mean detecting a word. It means knowing when that reference is really a useful item, when an amount is linked to a relevant line and when a match should be discarded. The challenge is not to extract “more text”, but to produce an output that is more reliable, more readable and more actionable.

The problem we are solving, therefore, is twofold. On the one hand, reducing the time needed to read and filter tender files. On the other, improving the quality of the first interpretation of the document, so that the person reviewing the tender already starts from a structured base. The tool does not replace professional judgment, but it removes a large part of the mechanical work and improves the consistency of the analysis.

The technical difficulties have been significant. We have had to distinguish between contracting documents and documents unrelated to the process, clean noisy outputs coming from the PDF, structure answers to very specific business questions and present information in a way that facilitates fast reading. It has also been necessary to incorporate a history of JSON outputs to compare iterations, review improvements and turn daily use itself into a learning source for the system. This is important because in this type of project improvement does not come only from development, but from the continuous observation of real cases.

The contribution to the organization is clear. First, it improves the speed of preliminary opportunity analysis. Second, it brings consistency: different documents can now be read with a common structure. Third, it reduces dependence on a completely manual review in the early phases. And, perhaps most importantly, it turns the dispersed knowledge of “what we should look for in a tender” into an explicit, reusable and improvable operational criterion.

This project shows that useful document automation is not just about digitizing files, but about translating complex documentation into understandable decisions. Real value appears when technology adapts to the way the organization works, to its real questions and to its commercial and technical priorities. That is the path we are following: moving from document to data, and from data to a faster, clearer and better founded decision.
